# Frontend Design and Implementation of the Dawava Pharmacy Portal

## 1. Introduction

Independent and small-chain pharmacies frequently operate without a unified digital system for the administrative and operational tasks that surround the pharmacy itself: registering a new pharmacy business with a platform or regulator, coordinating a growing network of physical branches, and communicating internally between staff working across those branches. These tasks are commonly handled through disconnected, manual processes rather than a single, purpose-built system. The Dawava Pharmacy Portal was developed to bring these tasks together into one coherent platform, and this chapter presents the design and implementation of its client-side subsystem — the single-page web application that provides the entire user-facing experience of the system and communicates with a set of backend services through well-defined network interfaces. The backend services the client consumes are treated in this chapter as external systems, addressed only insofar as their contracts and behavior shaped the design of the client itself.

The frontend serves four distinct user personas, each with a different relationship to the platform. A prospective pharmacy owner — referred to throughout this chapter as the *Applicant* — approaches the system before holding any account at all, and is guided through a structured registration process in which the pharmacy's organizational details, its physical branches, its ownership, and its supporting legal documentation are collected for review. Once an application has been approved, three authenticated operational roles emerge. The *System Administrator* is an internal platform operator responsible for reviewing incoming applications and deciding whether a new pharmacy is admitted to the platform, and for maintaining the platform-wide roles and permissions described in Section 5.9. The *Pharmacy Manager* is the operator of an approved pharmacy, holding full administrative control over that pharmacy's identity, its network of branches, its staff, and its pharmacy-scoped roles, in addition to every capability available to pharmacy staff. *Pharmacy Staff* are individual employees working within a specific branch, with day-to-day access to that branch's operational dashboard, its inventory and sales information, and the platform's internal communication tools. Each of these roles is served by a distinct, access-controlled area of the application, all built on a shared architectural and visual foundation described later in this chapter.

The objectives pursued in the design of this subsystem were sixfold: to provide a resilient, guided registration experience that a new pharmacy can complete without assistance and can safely resume if interrupted; to give the platform's administrators a clear, auditable workflow for reviewing and deciding on incoming applications; to equip managers and staff with dashboards appropriate to their working context, oriented around the branch they are currently operating in, and with visibility into that branch's inventory and sales activity; to give a pharmacy manager a self-service console through which the pharmacy's identity, its branch network, and its staff can be administered, including interactive, map-based control over each branch's physical location and a shareable invitation mechanism for bringing new staff onto the platform; to give both the platform and each individual pharmacy a coherent way to define who is permitted to do what, through a role-and-permission model administered at two levels; and to provide pharmacy personnel with a real-time messaging capability, integrated directly into the platform they already use for their daily work, rather than requiring a separate, general-purpose tool. Authentication, authorization, and the protection of a user's session were treated as first-class concerns running through every one of these objectives, rather than as a layer applied on top of them after the fact.

Underlying all four of these personas is a common operational reality that the design of the system had to take seriously from the outset: a pharmacy is rarely a single, self-contained location. As a pharmacy grows, it typically does so by opening additional branches, each with its own address, its own staff, and its own day-to-day operational picture, while still belonging to one legal pharmacy entity and one management structure above it. A system intended to support this kind of business therefore needs to represent both levels at once — the pharmacy as a whole, and each branch individually — and to let a user move between the branches they are responsible for without friction. This dual notion of identity, at the level of the pharmacy and at the level of the branch, recurs throughout the chapter, most visibly in the branch-management and workspace-switching capabilities described in Section 5.

The scope of this chapter is the frontend client itself: an Angular single-page application consuming a small number of backend services reached over conventional web protocols. Beyond the core registration, administration, and communication capabilities already outlined above, the platform additionally supports branch-level inventory and sales oversight, the administration and invitation of pharmacy staff, and two parallel systems for defining and assigning roles and permissions — one operated by the System Administrator at the level of the whole platform, and one operated by each Pharmacy Manager at the level of their own pharmacy. Each of these capabilities is built on the same architectural foundation described throughout this chapter and is presented in Section 5 alongside the modules already introduced. The remainder of this chapter is organized as follows. Section 2 summarizes the technological foundations on which the client is built. Section 3 describes its overall and internal architecture. Section 4 details authentication, authorization, and security. Section 5 walks through the implementation of each functional module in turn. Section 6 focuses on API integration and data communication. Section 7 discusses the user interface and responsive design strategy. Section 8 reflects on notable implementation decisions and their rationale, and Section 9 closes with a summary of the chapter.

## 2. Technology Background

### 2.1 Single-Page Applications and Component-Based User Interfaces

The client is implemented as a **single-page application (SPA)**, an architectural style in which a single HTML document is loaded once and the visible interface is thereafter updated dynamically within the browser as the user navigates and interacts with it, rather than requesting a complete new document from the server for every transition [1]. This model suits a system such as this one particularly well: pharmacy staff and managers are expected to keep the application open for extended periods during a working shift, moving frequently between a dashboard, messaging, and administrative screens, and a single-page architecture allows those transitions to feel immediate rather than incurring a full page reload each time.

The application is built with **Angular**, a component-based framework in which the interface is composed of a tree of self-contained components, each pairing behavior with a corresponding view [1]. The version of Angular used in this project adopts a *standalone component* model, in which each component, directive, and view fragment declares its own dependencies directly rather than being grouped into larger enclosing modules. This has a direct architectural consequence explored further in Section 3: because a component no longer needs an enclosing module to be resolved before it can be used, individual screens can be loaded independently and on demand, keeping the amount of code a given user's browser needs to download proportionate to the parts of the application they actually visit.

### 2.2 Reactive State Management

Application state is managed through two complementary reactive mechanisms that serve distinct purposes. **Signals** are synchronous, observable values that automatically notify every part of the interface that depends on them whenever they change, without requiring an explicit subscription-and-cleanup lifecycle around each dependency [2]. Signals are used throughout the client as the primary means of holding state that is shared across more than one screen — the identity of the signed-in user, the branch and pharmacy currently active in the session, and the data collected so far during an in-progress registration are all examples of such state. **RxJS**, a library for composing asynchronous event streams declaratively, is used to express the arrival of data over time — network responses, and the stream of real-time events described in Section 2.3 — as pipelines that transform, combine, and ultimately feed into the signals described above [3].

This division of labor was chosen deliberately for an application of this shape: the great majority of shared state in this system is naturally owned by one clearly identifiable part of the application, making a lightweight, service-owned signal a proportionate way to hold it, while the genuinely asynchronous, event-driven aspects of the system — a request that may fail and be retried, or a continuous stream of real-time messaging events — are better expressed through RxJS's richer set of composition operators. The two mechanisms interoperate at a small number of well-defined points, such as when a piece of interface logic needs to wait for a signal to reach a particular value before proceeding, or when the latest value of an asynchronous stream needs to be exposed to the rest of the interface as an ordinary, readable signal.

### 2.3 Client–Server Communication Patterns

Two complementary communication patterns are used, matched to the nature of each interaction. The great majority of the client's interactions with the backend — creating a pharmacy application, retrieving a user's profile, editing a branch — follow the **Representational State Transfer (REST)** architectural style, in which resources are addressed by location and manipulated through a small, standard set of operations, with structured, typically JSON-encoded request and response bodies [4]. REST's request/response model is well suited to these interactions because each one is initiated by the user and has a single, well-defined outcome.

The platform's messaging module, by contrast, is inherently bidirectional: a message, a typing indicator, or a change in another participant's online status must reach the interface without the user having explicitly asked for it. For this class of interaction, the client instead maintains a persistent, full-duplex connection using the **WebSocket** protocol, wrapped by the **Socket.IO** library, which layers a reliable, event-based messaging abstraction, automatic reconnection, and a fallback transport on top of the underlying connection [5]. Using a persistent connection for messaging, rather than having the client repeatedly ask the server whether anything new has happened, keeps the messaging experience close to instantaneous while avoiding the overhead a purely request-driven approach would introduce at that frequency.

The two patterns are not treated as alternatives competing for the same role but as complements addressing genuinely different needs within the same module. The messaging module, in particular, draws on both at once: the request-based channel is used to retrieve conversation history and to perform actions with a clear, singular outcome, such as sending a specific message, while the persistent connection is reserved for the continuous stream of events — new messages arriving, a participant starting to type, a message being marked as read — that have no natural single point at which the client could have simply asked for them. This division is described in more detail as it applies to the communication module in Section 5.5.

### 2.4 Identity and Access Technology

Authentication and authorization throughout the platform are based on **JSON Web Tokens (JWT)**, a compact, digitally signed token format capable of carrying identity and contextual claims in a self-contained, verifiable structure [6]. A key property of this format, exploited directly by the client, is that meaningful information — such as which branch and pharmacy a session is currently scoped to — can be read directly from a token already held by the browser, without an additional request purely to establish that context. This keeps the client responsive immediately after signing in, and is described further, together with the token-renewal strategy built around it, in Section 4.

### 2.5 Geolocation and Rich Media Capture

Physical branch locations, wherever they appear throughout the application, are captured and displayed using **Leaflet**, an interactive mapping library, rendering map tiles supplied by the **OpenStreetMap** project [7], [8]. Given that a pharmacy operator managing several branches needs to be confident that each branch's registered address corresponds to its true physical location — both for internal administration and for a customer trying to find the nearest branch — an interactive, visually verifiable map was judged preferable to a text-only address field throughout the registration and branch-management workflows described in Section 5.

The platform's messaging module additionally makes use of the browser's native audio-capture capability, which grants temporary access to the device's microphone and produces a compressed audio recording suitable for upload and playback [9]. Supporting voice messages directly within the platform's own communication tool, rather than requiring staff to fall back on an external application for anything beyond typed text, was considered important to the goal of keeping day-to-day pharmacy communication inside a single, purpose-built system.

### 2.6 Visual Design Foundations and Backend Organization

The application's visual language is implemented with a utility-first styling approach built on **Tailwind CSS**, layered over a small set of centrally defined design tokens that govern color, spacing, and both light and dark presentation, discussed further in Section 7 [10]. On the server side, the platform is organized as a small number of independently addressable services rather than a single monolithic backend — an identity and core-resource service, a file-handling service, and a real-time communication service — an instance of the **microservice-oriented** style of backend organization, in which each service is scoped to a cohesive area of responsibility and can evolve independently of the others [11]. This organization is not merely a backend concern: it shaped several of the client's own architectural boundaries, described in Section 3, since the client's own service layer mirrors this same division, with each client-side service addressing exactly one of these backend areas.

## 3. System Architecture

### 3.1 Overall Architecture

The platform follows a client–server architecture in which the frontend holds no durable business data of its own beyond what it caches locally for the duration of a session. All persistent state — user accounts, pharmacy and branch records, applications and their supporting documents, staff assignments and invitations, roles and permissions, inventory and transaction records, and conversation history — is owned by the backend and reached through three logically distinct services: an identity and core-resource service handling authentication, user and pharmacy data, applications, staff, inventory, and roles; a file-handling service responsible for document and photograph storage; and a real-time communication service handling messaging, reached both through conventional requests for history and management, and through the persistent connection described in Section 2.3. The client is configured, at build time, with the network location of each of these services, so that the same application code can be deployed against a local development environment or a production environment purely by changing that configuration, with no change to the application logic itself. ### 3.2 Frontend Architecture

Internally, the client is organized by **feature** rather than by technical layer, so that the components, services, and navigational structure belonging to one business capability are kept together and can be developed, understood, and loaded largely independently of the rest of the application. A small **core layer** provides authentication state, route protection, and other cross-cutting services used throughout the application. A **shared layer** sits above it, holding domain services and presentational components that are genuinely reused by more than one feature — the pharmacy and branch data services and the interactive map component are the clearest examples. An **application shell** supplies the persistent navigation chrome common to every authenticated screen, described further in Section 5.6. Finally, a set of **feature modules** — registration, administration, manager operations, staff operations, account settings, inventory, sales, and communication — each provide one coherent capability, built on top of the core and shared layers beneath them.

This layering keeps the dependency direction predominantly one-directional — features depend on the shared and core layers, never the reverse — with a small number of deliberate exceptions: the administrator's application-review screens draw on the same underlying application data model used by the registration module, since both ultimately observe and act upon the same application records as they move through the review process, and the staff-management screen described in Section 5.8 is shared, unmodified, between the administrator and manager areas of the application, since reviewing and administering the staff at a branch is the same underlying task regardless of which of the two roles is performing it. These were conscious choices in favor of a single, unambiguous source of truth for shared data and shared behavior, rather than maintaining independently evolving copies of the same logic across otherwise separate features. Organizing the client this way also has a practical benefit beyond the runtime behavior it produces: it keeps the codebase itself easy to reason about as the platform grows. A developer working on the messaging module, for instance, can be confident that the great majority of the code relevant to that work lives within the communication feature and the small set of shared and core services it depends on, rather than being spread unpredictably across the rest of the application. The same property makes it straightforward to introduce a further feature module in the future, following the same established pattern, without disturbing the modules that already exist — the inventory, sales, and role-management modules described in Sections 5.7 through 5.9 were each added to the application in exactly this way.

### 3.3 Navigation and Routing

Navigation is organized into three role-scoped areas — administrator, manager, and staff — each protected so that only an authenticated user holding the matching role may enter it, alongside a separate registration area used by applicants prior to approval and largely governed by the state of their own in-progress application rather than by a role at all, since an applicant does not yet hold one, and a standalone invitation-acceptance page, described in Section 5.8, reached from outside any of the three role-scoped areas. Within each role-scoped area, a persistent set of navigational destinations is presented consistently, reflecting the working areas relevant to that role — for a manager or staff member, for instance, a dashboard, inventory and sales oversight, messaging, staff administration, reporting, and account settings; for an administrator, a dashboard, pharmacy-application review, staff administration, and the platform-wide roles and permissions catalog described in Section 5.9.

Every screen in the application is loaded on demand, the first time its corresponding destination is actually visited, rather than being bundled into the code downloaded when the application first starts. This has a direct, practical benefit for a multi-role system such as this one: a member of staff, for example, never downloads the code belonging to the administrator's application-review screens, since their session never activates that part of the application at all. The result is that the amount of code any given user's browser has to fetch and execute stays proportionate to the parts of the system that user actually uses, rather than to the system as a whole.

### 3.4 Shared State Ownership

State that more than one screen depends on — the authenticated user's identity and role, their active branch and pharmacy context, and, during registration, the data collected so far — is each owned by exactly one application-wide reactive service, rather than being duplicated or passed explicitly between the screens that need it. Because every part of the interface that depends on a given piece of shared state ultimately reads it from that same underlying source, a change made in one screen is reflected everywhere else that piece of state is represented, without any explicit signalling being written between the two. Updating a branch's photograph from the account-settings area, for instance, is immediately reflected in the navigation bar's avatar and in the workspace switcher's own view of that branch, purely because both read the same underlying value rather than a snapshot copy of it. This pattern, applied consistently, is what allows the interface to remain internally consistent as a user moves between otherwise unrelated screens, and is referred to at several points later in this chapter wherever it is the mechanism behind a piece of interface behavior.

## 4. Authentication, Authorization, and Security

### 4.1 Login and Role Determination

A user authenticates by submitting an identifier and a password through a validated sign-in form. On successful verification, the backend issues a short-lived access token and a longer-lived refresh token, together with information describing which branches the account is permitted to operate under. The client persists both tokens locally and inspects the freshly issued access token directly, as described in Section 2.4, to determine the branch and pharmacy the session is initially scoped to, avoiding an additional round trip purely to establish that context before the user's dashboard can be shown.

The user's operating role — administrator, manager, or staff — is then determined from the combination of information available about the account. The client considers several potential sources of this information, in order of how authoritative they are judged to be: explicit indicators on the user's own profile record are consulted first; where these are not conclusive, information carried within the token itself is used instead; and an account for which no more specific role can be established is treated as staff by default, the least privileged of the three roles, so that ambiguity is always resolved conservatively rather than in favor of broader access. This layered approach exists because the account information returned by the backend has, over the course of the platform's development, not always taken a single, entirely uniform shape, and resolving role in this ordered fashion allows the client to remain correct regardless of exactly which of these sources happens to be populated for a given account. Once the role is known, the user is taken directly to the dashboard appropriate to that role. Basing this decision on a token-based scheme rather than, for instance, a server-maintained session held purely on the backend was also a deliberate architectural choice, consistent with the broader, service-oriented organization of the backend described in Section 2.6. A token that is self-contained and independently verifiable lets any of the platform's backend services confirm a request's identity and role without needing to consult a central session store on every single request, which suits a backend organized as several independent services rather than one. From the client's perspective, the practical benefit is the one already described: the identity and workspace context needed to render the very first screen after sign-in are available the instant the token is received, rather than depending on a further round trip.

### 4.2 Session Continuity and Transparent Renewal

Because a single-page application's in-memory state is lost whenever the browser is fully reloaded, the client re-establishes a previously authenticated session automatically at start-up whenever a valid refresh token is still available, restoring the user's identity and role and returning them directly to the dashboard appropriate to that role, rather than momentarily showing an unauthenticated view before redirecting.

During normal use, every outgoing request to a protected resource passes through a single point in the application responsible for attaching the current access token to that request. Should a request be rejected because that token has since expired, the client transparently obtains a new token pair from the backend and retries the original request once with the new token, so that an expired access token is, in the ordinary case, entirely invisible to the person using the application — no error is shown, and no action is required of them. Because several requests can fail for this same reason within a short window of one another, such concurrent failures are coalesced onto a single renewal attempt rather than each independently requesting a new token pair, avoiding unnecessary duplicate work at the exact moment the session is already under some strain. Only if the refresh token itself has expired or been revoked — for instance, following a deliberate sign-out from that device, described in Section 4.4 — is the user finally returned to the sign-in screen.

### 4.3 Route Protection and Role-Based Access

Each role-scoped area of the application described in Section 3.3 is protected by two checks, applied once at the entry point of that area rather than repeated on every individual screen within it. The first is a session check, confirming only that the visitor holds valid credentials at all; the second is a role check, confirming that those credentials belong to the specific role the area is reserved for. A user who is authenticated but holds the wrong role for a given area is redirected to their own dashboard rather than shown an error page, preserving a smooth experience even in the case of a mistyped or bookmarked link; a user who is not authenticated at all is returned to the sign-in screen instead.

Because the user's role is sometimes still being resolved at the exact moment a route-level check runs — most notably immediately after the automatic session-restoration described in Section 4.2, which itself involves a brief round trip to the backend — the role check is written to tolerate this timing rather than assume the role is always already known. It waits, for a bounded period, for that resolution to complete before making its decision, so that a legitimately authenticated user is never incorrectly denied access simply because their role had not yet finished resolving at that particular instant. ### 4.4 Session Management and Account Recovery

Beyond the token pair that drives everyday authentication, the platform models a user's authenticated presence more broadly as a set of independent sessions, one per signed-in device, which the user can review directly from within the account-settings area. For each active session, the interface shows the approximate device and browser it belongs to, the location from which it last connected, and when it was last active, giving a user a concrete picture of everywhere their account is currently signed in. A session can be revoked individually at any time, or every session can be ended at once following a password re-confirmation, a deliberately stronger requirement given the broader impact of that particular action. Revoking a session that happens to be the one currently in use ends the local session immediately, since the corresponding credentials have, by that point, already been invalidated on the server as a direct consequence of the revoke action itself.

Password recovery, reached separately from the main sign-in flow, is implemented as a three-stage process. The user first supplies their registered email address; a numeric verification code is then sent to that address and must be entered correctly before the process can continue; and finally a new password is chosen, validated for a minimum length and a mix of character types before it can be submitted. To avoid revealing to an outside party whether a given email address is registered on the platform at all, the interface proceeds to the verification stage regardless of whether the initial request for a code succeeds or fails, deferring any meaningful confirmation or rejection of the attempt to the verification stage itself, where a genuinely incorrect or expired code is the only thing that halts progress.

### 4.5 Security Considerations

Several concrete security properties follow directly from the design described in this section, considered here against the categories of risk identified in the OWASP Top Ten guidance for web application security [12]. Authentication tokens are held in the browser's local storage rather than in a cookie, a common pattern for single-page applications of this kind that avoids the cross-site request-forgery risks associated with cookie-based authentication, at the cost of relying on the application's own defenses against script injection to keep those tokens from being read by an unintended party. Access to administrative and management capabilities is restricted both at the level of route entry, described in Section 4.3, and within screens that are shared between roles, consistent with a least-privilege presentation of the interface in which a user is never shown a control they are not entitled to use — while the true authorization boundary for any action that actually changes data is understood to be enforced by the backend independently of what the client happens to display, since the interface's own checks exist primarily for usability rather than as the system's sole safeguard.

The multi-device session visibility described in Section 4.4 gives a user direct, immediate means of responding to a lost or stolen device, or to a suspected compromise of their credentials, without necessarily needing to change their password at the same time, since the affected session alone can be revoked. Finally, destructive or broad-impact actions throughout the application — removing a pharmacy or a branch, deleting a role that may already be assigned to staff, or ending every active session at once — each require an explicit secondary confirmation appropriate to their impact before being carried out, reducing the likelihood that such an action is taken unintentionally.

The client also benefits from a set of protections inherent to the framework it is built on. Angular renders dynamic content into the page through a templating mechanism that escapes untrusted values by default, so that data originating from a user or from the backend is not inadvertently interpreted as executable markup when it is displayed elsewhere in the interface — for instance, a pharmacy's display name shown throughout the shell, or a message's text content shown within a conversation. This default behavior is relied upon rather than overridden, so that the burden of guarding against this class of issue does not fall on every individual screen that happens to render user-supplied content.

## 5. Core Modules and Feature Implementation

### 5.1 Registration and Onboarding

The registration module guides a prospective pharmacy owner through a structured, five-stage process: organizational details, one or more physical branches with map-based geolocation, ownership and tax information, supporting legal documents, and a final review before submission. Each stage validates its own fields before the applicant is allowed to proceed to the next, so that a malformed submission is caught at the point it is entered rather than surfacing later as a rejected application. The data entered at each stage is preserved locally on the applicant's device as they move between stages, so that closing the browser or returning to the process later resumes it from where it was left, rather than requiring it to be started over from the beginning. Because the application's true status is always ultimately confirmed against the backend rather than trusted purely from what has been cached locally, the wizard is able to reconcile itself correctly even where the application's status has changed elsewhere in the meantime — for example, following a decision made by an administrator — by the time the applicant next opens the process.

The branch stage of the wizard supports more than one branch being registered under the same application, each entered through an identical set of fields — a name, an address, a city, and a contact number — paired with an interactive map through which that branch's precise location can be selected. Four different ways of setting a location converge on the same underlying pair of coordinates: dragging a marker directly on the map, tapping a location on the map itself, entering coordinates manually, or, where the browser permits it, using the device's own current location as a starting point. Whichever method is used, the resulting coordinates are validated to fall within a genuinely valid geographic range before the applicant can proceed, and are carried forward through the remainder of the wizard and, ultimately, into the branch's permanent record once the application is approved.

The ownership stage collects the information needed to establish who legally owns and is accountable for the pharmacy — full name, contact details, the legal form under which the business is organized, and a tax identification number — while the documents stage requires the applicant to supply the pharmacy's operating license and a form of the owner's identification, each checked for an acceptable file type and a reasonable maximum size before being uploaded using the platform's standard file-handling mechanism, described further in Section 6. Contact numbers collected throughout the wizard are validated against the applicable regional phone-numbering convention, and email addresses and other structured fields are similarly checked for a plausible, well-formed value before the applicant is allowed to proceed, so that avoidable data-quality issues are caught during registration itself rather than surfacing later during administrative review.

Once submitted, the applicant is shown one of three outcome screens, determined by the application's current status: a review-in-progress screen, which checks periodically for a decision and transitions the applicant automatically to the appropriate outcome the moment one is recorded, without requiring the applicant to check back manually; an approval screen, from which the newly established pharmacy manager proceeds to sign in and begin managing their pharmacy; and a rejection screen, which surfaces the reviewing administrator's stated reason where one was provided and offers the applicant a direct path to begin a new application should they wish to. ### 5.2 Application Review and Approval

The system administrator is presented with a consolidated listing of every submitted application, each shown with its identifying details and current status, and can search and filter that listing directly to locate a specific application quickly as the number of submissions grows. Opening any entry in the listing brings up a detailed review screen, presenting the applicant's organizational, ownership, and branch information alongside every supporting document that was submitted, each of which can be opened directly for inspection from within that same screen without navigating away from the review.

From this detailed view, the administrator may record a decision on any application that is still awaiting one: approving it, which grants the applicant's account manager-level access to the newly established pharmacy and its first registered branch, or rejecting it, which records an optional written reason that is later surfaced to the applicant on their own outcome screen. Once a decision has been recorded for a given application, the approve and reject actions are no longer offered for it, so that an application cannot be acted on a second time once it has already been settled — a safeguard against an accidental duplicate decision on the same application. Figure 8 depicts this review-and-decision workflow in full, from the initial listing through to the resulting status update.

This review process was deliberately kept close in structure to the registration process it evaluates: the same organizational, ownership, and branch information the applicant entered is presented back to the administrator in the same shape it was submitted, so that the reviewer is assessing exactly what the applicant provided rather than a reinterpreted summary of it. Because an approval decision has an immediate and consequential effect — the creation of a new pharmacy account with management access — the action is confirmed explicitly before being carried out, consistent with the confirmation convention applied to other high-impact actions described in Section 4.5 and Section 7.2.

A manager's dashboard additionally surfaces a summary of staffing across the branch as a whole, reflecting the broader supervisory responsibility that comes with that role, whereas a staff member's dashboard is oriented more narrowly around the immediate, personal working context of a single shift. ### 5.4 Pharmacy and Branch Management

The account-settings area is where a pharmacy's identity and its network of branches are administered, and is organized into a small number of clearly separated sections reflecting the different kinds of information a user might need to manage there. Every signed-in user, regardless of role, can view their own account details from this area and change their password, with session management handled as described already in Section 4.4 rather than repeated here.

Staff members, by contrast, view the pharmacy and branch information relevant to where they work in a read-only form within this same area, without the editing controls described next — a deliberate reflection of the fact that responsibility for the pharmacy's legal identity and its branch network rests with its manager, while staff continue to see the same underlying information whenever they need to reference it, such as the branch's address or its opening hours.

For a pharmacy manager specifically, the same area additionally exposes a broader set of administrative controls. The pharmacy's registered legal name, display name, and photograph can be edited directly, and the manager can browse the pharmacy's full list of branches to designate which one a future sign-in should land on by default — a setting distinct from simply switching the branch active in the current session, described in Section 5.6, since it governs where the *next* sign-in begins rather than the session already under way. Individual branches can be created, edited, or removed entirely from this same area, each with its own address, contact details, and opening hours, an interactive map for setting its precise geolocation using the same location-selection methods available during registration, and a dedicated photograph of its own. Removing or substantially reconfiguring the branch currently active in the session transitions the user smoothly to another available branch, where one exists, rather than leaving the session in an inconsistent state. Every field entered across these forms is validated before it can be saved — geographic coordinates, in particular, are checked to fall within a genuinely valid range before a branch's location can be recorded. ### 5.5 Real-Time Communication

The communication module provides direct messaging between pharmacy personnel, combining a conventional, request-based interface for history and management with the persistent, real-time connection introduced in Section 2.3. A conversation is presented as a single, continuous, chronologically ordered thread that can be scrolled back through earlier history as needed, with older messages fetched progressively as the user scrolls rather than the entire history being loaded at once. A message exchanged within that thread may carry plain text, an image or general file attachment, or a voice recording captured directly through the browser's microphone access, delivered as a compressed audio clip that the recipient can play back through an inline, seekable player without leaving the conversation. Messages a user has sent themselves can be edited after the fact or withdrawn entirely, may be replied to directly in the context of an earlier message, and may be reacted to with an emoji — a set of conventions intentionally modeled on the interaction patterns familiar from mainstream consumer messaging applications, so that the module feels immediately familiar to a first-time user.

Beyond the exchange of messages themselves, participants in a conversation see a live typing indicator and a live voice-recording indicator whenever the other party is composing a reply, an online-or-offline presence indicator for the other participant, and delivery and read receipts for each message they have sent — all driven directly by the real-time connection described in Section 2.3, rather than by the interface periodically asking the server whether anything has changed. To keep the experience responsive under ordinary network conditions, a message the current user sends is shown in the thread immediately, before the network request confirming it has actually completed, and is reconciled quietly against the server's authoritative copy of that message once confirmation — or the corresponding real-time event — arrives a moment later, so the sender never perceives a delay between pressing send and seeing their message appear.

A further nuance of the module reflects the platform's organizational model directly: because a branch is, in certain conversations, represented as a single shared identity covering more than one staff member working at that branch, a manager viewing such a conversation additionally sees which specific staff member authored any given message sent under that shared identity, while the other participant in the conversation continues to see only the branch as a whole, preserving a single, consistent point of contact from that participant's perspective while still giving the manager internal visibility into who on their team is handling the conversation. ### 5.6 Application Shell and Workspace Switching

Every authenticated screen in the application is framed by a common application shell, providing role-appropriate navigation, a general search entry point, a light/dark theme control, and an account menu, so that the surrounding chrome of the application remains entirely consistent as the user moves between otherwise very different screens. This consistency was considered particularly important given how often a user is expected to move between screens over the course of a working session, and how disruptive it would be for the surrounding frame of the application to shift each time.

For an account with access to more than one branch — whether because a manager oversees several branches under one pharmacy, or because a single individual holds a role at more than one pharmacy — the shell's top bar additionally offers a workspace switcher, through which the user can move between branches, and where relevant between pharmacies entirely, without needing to sign out and sign back in. The rest of the interface updates to reflect the newly active branch as soon as the switch completes, following directly from the shared-state design already described in Section 3.4: the dashboard, the account-settings area, the inventory and sales views, and the messaging module all immediately reflect the branch the user has just switched into, without any of those screens needing to be reloaded individually. ### 5.7 Inventory and Sales Management

Both the pharmacy manager and pharmacy staff are given a consolidated view of a branch's stock and its recent transaction activity, reached from the same navigation used throughout the rest of the application and scoped, in every case, to whichever branch is currently active in the session. The inventory area presents a summary of the active branch's stock position — the total number of distinct items carried, their combined value, and how many currently fall into each availability band — alongside a searchable, filterable, paginated listing of individual items, each shown with its identifying details, current quantity, price, and the time it was last updated. A status filter, together with a toggle to exclude items that are currently out of stock, lets a user narrow this listing to whatever subset is operationally relevant at the time. Because this view is scoped to the branch currently active in the session, switching branches through the workspace switcher described in Section 5.6 automatically refreshes it to reflect the newly selected branch, without any further action being required.

The sales area presents the same branch's recent transaction history in a comparable form: a set of summary figures — the total number of transactions recorded, the quantity added and reduced, the resulting net financial effect, and how many items moved into an out-of-stock state — followed by a searchable, filterable, paginated table of individual transactions, each showing the item involved, the source of the change, the quantity adjustment, the resulting change in stock status, its monetary value, and when it occurred. A transaction can be filtered by its source, by a date range, or by a free-text search, in the same manner as the inventory listing. One or more transactions can be selected directly from this table and printed as a formatted receipt, generated entirely within the browser and suitable for a standard or thermal printer, giving pharmacy staff a direct way to produce a receipt from an existing transaction record without leaving the application.

Both the inventory and sales listings can be exported for use outside the application: the inventory listing can be downloaded as a formatted, platform-branded PDF or as a spreadsheet reflecting whatever filters are currently applied, while the underlying transaction data can be downloaded as a CSV file directly from the backend. These exports and the receipt-printing capability are generated using standard, well-established client-side document-generation techniques, requiring no separate reporting tool. ### 5.8 Staff Management and Invitations

Both the System Administrator and the Pharmacy Manager are given a shared staff-management screen, reached from their respective navigation areas, through which the people working at a branch — and their access to it — can be reviewed and administered. The screen opens with a short set of summary figures covering the pharmacy's total staff count and how many are currently active or inactive at the branch in view, followed by a searchable, filterable, paginated listing of every staff assignment at that branch: each entry shows the staff member's profile, contact details, assigned role, status, and the date they joined, together with actions to change their role, temporarily deactivate their access, or, where access has already been deactivated, restore it. A separate view within the same screen lists any invitations currently pending, described next.

New staff are brought onto a branch in one of two ways. Where the person is already known to the pharmacy — for instance, someone already working at a different branch under the same pharmacy — they can be assigned directly to the branch in view, given a role and an optional note explaining the assignment. Where the person does not yet have any presence in the pharmacy, a shareable invitation link can instead be generated, scoped to a chosen role and to one or more branches, with a configurable expiry period. The generated link is presented for the manager or administrator to copy and share directly with the intended recipient, and every invitation issued this way remains visible and revocable from the pending-invitations view until it is either used or allowed to expire. ## 6. API Integration and Data Communication

### 6.1 Service Layer Conventions

Every interaction between the client and the backend is expressed through a small, dedicated service scoped to one resource area, so that the details of constructing a request, interpreting its response, and normalizing that response into a form the rest of the application can use are concentrated in one place rather than scattered across the many screens that ultimately depend on that data. This convention has proved useful in practice because backend responses are not always shaped with perfect uniformity across every endpoint; each client-side service is written to tolerantly interpret whichever reasonable shape of response it actually receives, rather than assuming one single, fixed structure, which has kept the interface stable in the face of such variation without requiring changes to every screen that happens to display that data.

### 6.2 Backend Service Segmentation

Each of the three backend services introduced in Section 3.1 is addressed by the client through its own configured network location, so that a request concerning identity, pharmacy, staffing, or role data, a file operation, and a messaging operation are each automatically routed to the appropriate service without the rest of the application needing any awareness of the details of that routing. This segmentation is preserved faithfully on the client side rather than being hidden behind a single, artificially uniform interface, since doing so would risk obscuring genuine differences in how each underlying service is organized and would make it harder to reason about which part of the backend a given piece of client code actually depends on.

### 6.3 File Handling

Documents and photographs follow a consistent, two-phase handling pattern wherever they occur across the application — during registration, in pharmacy and branch management, and as attachments within the messaging module. A file selected by the user is first validated for an acceptable type and a reasonable maximum size, and is then uploaded to the file-handling service, which returns a reference to the newly stored file. That reference is then attached to whatever record the file actually belongs to — an application's list of supporting documents, a pharmacy or branch's photograph, or a message's list of attachments — as an ordinary part of saving that record, rather than the file itself being embedded directly within it. Displaying a previously uploaded file follows the same pattern in reverse: immediately before a photograph or attachment needs to be shown, the client resolves a viewable link for that stored file through an authenticated request, rather than linking to it directly, since retrieving a stored file requires the same credentials as any other protected resource in the system. ### 6.4 Error Handling

Error handling follows a similarly consistent convention throughout the application: each interactive form or action maintains its own transient error state, populated with a message drawn from the failed response where the backend has supplied one, and a reasonable, context-appropriate default otherwise. This keeps error feedback local to the specific action that failed — the user sees clearly what went wrong and can correct it and try again without losing their place in the surrounding screen, and without an unrelated part of the application being disturbed by a failure that has nothing to do with it.

## 7. User Interface and Responsive Design

### 7.1 Visual Design and Theming

The interface's visual identity — a teal- and blue-accented palette set against neutral backgrounds — is expressed through a small set of centrally defined design tokens rather than through a third-party component theme applied wholesale, giving every part of the application, from the navigation shell down to an individual message bubble, a single, consistently applied source of truth for color, spacing, and structure. A light/dark theme control lets the user switch presentation modes at any time from the account menu described in Section 5.6; the choice made is remembered across future visits, and, the first time the application is used, defaults to the visitor's own system-level preference where one can be detected, so that a new user is not presented with a jarring default that conflicts with how the rest of their device is configured.

### 7.2 Interaction Patterns

A number of interaction conventions recur deliberately across otherwise unrelated parts of the application, reflecting a single, consistent design language rather than a series of isolated, one-off choices made independently within each module. Destructive actions require an explicit secondary confirmation appropriate to the scale of their impact — a lighter, inline confirmation for removing a single branch, for instance, against the heavier requirement of re-entering a password before every active session can be ended at once. Photographs displayed throughout the interface can be opened in a full-screen preview, dismissible equally by clicking outside the image, by an explicit close control, or by the keyboard's escape key, so that the same expectation about how to close such an overlay holds regardless of where in the application it is encountered. Every action that depends on a network request presents a clear, temporarily disabled, in-progress state for the duration of that request, preventing the same action from being triggered a second time while the first attempt is still being processed.

### 7.3 Responsive Layout

The application shell adapts from a persistent, two-column desktop layout — navigation shown alongside content at all times — to a single-column mobile layout in which navigation is tucked behind a menu control and presented as a temporary overlay only when opened, so that the full width of a smaller screen remains available for content the rest of the time. The communication module carries this adaptation furthest of any part of the application: on a narrower screen, the conversation list and an open conversation, which sit side by side on a larger display, instead become two full-width panels that the user moves between one at a time, with a dedicated control provided to return from an open conversation back to the list — a pattern deliberately familiar from mobile messaging applications more broadly, chosen so that the module requires no real adjustment period for a user already accustomed to that convention elsewhere. Throughout the application, the interactive maps described in Sections 5.1 and 5.4 resize themselves correctly as their surrounding layout changes, so that a map embedded within a card or a form remains fully usable across this entire range of screen sizes rather than only at the width it happened to be initialized at.

Responsive behavior of this kind matters in practice for a system used by pharmacy staff, who are not necessarily seated at a desktop workstation for every interaction with the platform — a manager checking on a branch from a personal device outside working hours, or a staff member glancing at a message between serving customers, are both realistic patterns of use the interface needed to support comfortably rather than merely tolerate. The same reasoning extends to the light/dark theme control described in Section 7.1: pharmacies frequently operate extended or late hours, and a dark presentation reduces glare and eye strain during evening or overnight use in a way a single fixed appearance could not.

## 8. Implementation Approach and Design Rationale

Several recurring design choices shaped the implementation described throughout this chapter, each reflecting a deliberate judgment about what best suited an application of this particular shape and scale. Shared application state is modeled as reactive signals owned by a small number of application-wide services, described in Section 3.4, rather than through a separate, centralized store layered on top of the rest of the application. This was judged proportionate to a system in which the great majority of shared state is naturally owned by one clearly identifiable part of the application already, making the additional structure a centralized store would introduce difficult to justify at this scale; the genuinely asynchronous aspects of the system, by contrast, continue to be expressed through composable request and event pipelines, with the two mechanisms bridged only at the specific points where one genuinely needs to interoperate with the other.

The consistent use of independently loadable, self-declaring components throughout the client, discussed in Section 2.1, keeps the boundary between features clean and observable directly in the structure of the codebase, and ensures, as a direct architectural consequence rather than an incidental one, that a user operating under one role never downloads the interface belonging to another role they do not hold. Form handling across the application is deliberately not standardized on a single approach: screens whose validation is more elaborate — involving pattern matching, checks that span more than one field at once, or a form whose number of fields can grow dynamically, as in the multi-branch stage of the registration wizard — make use of the framework's fuller, declarative validation facilities, while screens whose validation needs are comparatively straightforward, such as most of the account-settings, staff-management, and role-management areas, use a lighter, more direct approach proportionate to that simplicity rather than incurring the same structural overhead throughout.

Interactive features that benefit most from immediate feedback — sending a chat message, reacting to one, or staging a document during registration, each described in Sections 5.1 and 5.5 — update the visible interface before the corresponding network request has actually completed, and reconcile that update against the server's eventual, authoritative response once it arrives. This trades a small amount of additional bookkeeping within the affected screens for an interface that feels genuinely immediate under ordinary network conditions, a property judged particularly important for the real-time messaging module, where any perceptible delay between sending a message and seeing it appear would noticeably undermine the sense of a live conversation.

The decision to organize the backend as several independent services, described in Section 2.6, was mirrored deliberately on the client rather than abstracted away, because doing so keeps the boundaries of the system legible from the frontend's own code and not only from the backend's. A developer extending the client can tell, from the structure of the service layer alone, which backend capability a given piece of functionality ultimately depends on, which becomes increasingly valuable as more capabilities are added to the platform over time — as the inventory, sales, staff-management, and role-management modules described in Sections 5.7 through 5.9 each were — and reduces the risk of a change intended for one area of the system having an unexpected effect on another that happens to share client-side code purely by coincidence rather than by genuine relationship.

Finally, the correctness of the behaviors described throughout this chapter was established through a combination of automated unit tests targeting core service logic and systematic, scenario-based verification of complete user journeys — registration through to a recorded decision, branch administration, staff invitation through to an accepted assignment, role creation through to permission assignment, and message exchange between two authenticated sessions — each carried out end to end against a live instance of the backend environment rather than relying solely on isolated, component-level checks. This end-to-end style of verification was preferred precisely because several of the behaviors most central to this chapter — the reconciliation of an optimistic chat message against its authoritative counterpart, or the resumability of an interrupted registration — only genuinely manifest once the full path from interface to backend and back is exercised together, rather than being fully observable from any single layer of the system in isolation.

## 9. Chapter Summary

This chapter has presented the design and implementation of the frontend subsystem of the Dawava Pharmacy Portal: a feature-organized, signal-driven single-page application providing a guided, resilient pharmacy-registration experience with interactive, map-based branch geolocation; a complete administrator review-and-decision workflow supported by a searchable application listing and direct document inspection; role-appropriate dashboards oriented around a manager or staff member's currently active branch; token-based authentication with transparent renewal, layered role resolution, and comprehensive multi-device session management; a manager-facing console for administering a pharmacy's identity and its entire branch network, including interactive geolocation and photographic identity for each branch; branch-scoped visibility into inventory levels and sales/transaction history, with document export and receipt-printing built directly into the interface; a shared staff-management capability, available to both administrators and managers, through which staff can be assigned to a branch directly or brought on through a shareable, expiring invitation link; two parallel role-and-permission systems — one platform-wide, administered by the System Administrator, and one pharmacy-scoped, administered by each Pharmacy Manager — through which access to the platform's more sensitive capabilities is defined and assigned; and a real-time messaging system supporting text, image, file, and voice communication, complete with presence, typing, and read-receipt indicators and a branch-aware model of message attribution.

Together, these capabilities are delivered through a consistent architectural foundation established early in the chapter and referred to throughout it: a layered, feature-based structure with a clearly bounded set of cross-feature dependencies; shared application state owned centrally and consumed reactively wherever it is needed; a uniform pattern for backend communication and file handling applied identically across every module that touches either; and a visual and interaction language kept deliberately consistent from one screen to the next. This foundation has already shown itself capable of accommodating substantial new functionality — the inventory, sales, staff-management, and role-management modules described in this chapter were each added to the platform on top of the same core layers used by the modules that preceded them — and is well positioned to extend to the platform's further operational areas as the surrounding system continues to grow.
